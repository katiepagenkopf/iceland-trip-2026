Day 4

Jökulsárlón Glacier Lagoon

Where icebergs calve off Vatnajökull — Europe's largest glacier — and drift for years before melting into the sea. Blue, white, streaked with ash. The deepest lake in Iceland.

📍 Southeast Iceland, Route 1
Directions Photos

What it is

A glacial lagoon at the edge of Vatnajökull, Europe's largest glacier. Icebergs that calve off the glacier float here for years, slowly melting and rotating — blue, white, and sometimes streaked with black volcanic ash. Eventually they're small enough to wash through a short river channel out to the ocean.

The lagoon didn't exist until the 1930s. As Vatnajökull has retreated, the lagoon has grown — it's now the deepest lake in Iceland (over 930 feet). What you're looking at is climate change in real time, and it's hauntingly beautiful.

Good to know

When you arrive: Walk along the shoreline toward the river mouth. The best icebergs are often near where the lagoon meets the ocean. The colors shift constantly — what looks white from a distance reveals blues and crystal-clear ice up close.

Diamond Beach: Right across the road, chunks of ice wash up on jet-black volcanic sand. Some are crystal-clear, others glow blue. It's one of the most photographed spots in Iceland, and you'll understand why when you see it.

Seals: Look for harbor seals lounging on the icebergs or swimming in the lagoon. They're curious and often come close to the shore.

Safety: The ice can be unstable. Don't climb on icebergs near the water's edge, and keep a safe distance from the lagoon's edge — the underwater ice can shift suddenly.

Icelandic Culture Note

The lagoon's Icelandic name, Jökulsárlón, breaks down beautifully: jökull (glacier), á (river), lón (lagoon). Icelandic is one of the few languages that still builds new words from old roots rather than borrowing from English — so "telephone" becomes sími (old word for "thread"), and "computer" becomes tölva (a blend of tala "number" and völva "prophetess" — literally "number prophetess").

🇮🇸 Huldufólk (Hidden People)
Many Icelanders won't say they believe in huldufólk, but they won't say they don't either. Roads have been rerouted to avoid disturbing rocks where hidden people are said to live. Around Jökulsárlón, locals sometimes attribute the lagoon's ever-changing ice formations to the work of unseen forces. Whether you believe or not, there's something about this place that feels... watched. In the best way.

The Story Behind It

In the 1930s, a small pond formed at the foot of Breiðamerkurjökull glacier. By the 1970s, it had grown into a proper lagoon. Today it's nearly 10 square miles — and still expanding as the glacier retreats.

"The glacier that created this lagoon is the same one that carved the valleys you'll drive through today. It's been retreating for a century, and what it leaves behind is this: a graveyard of ice that's somehow the most alive place in Iceland."

The lagoon has appeared in several Hollywood films — Die Another Day, Batman Begins, and Tomb Raider all used it as a filming location. The Bond car chase across a frozen lagoon? That was here (with some movie magic).
