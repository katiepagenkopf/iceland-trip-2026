Hotel Siglunes

A family-run hotel on the harbor in Siglufjörður, with fjord views, an on-site restaurant, and a Moroccan-themed dinner menu that's become a destination in its own right. Small, personal, and right on the water.

📍 Hafnarbraut 5, 580 Siglufjörður
Directions Photos

What it is

A 24-room hotel right on Siglufjörður harbor, with views across the fjord to the mountains on the far side. The building is a converted fish processing facility — the owners gutted it, added floor-to-ceiling windows facing the water, and turned it into a comfortable base for exploring the Tröllaskagi peninsula. The rooms are simple and clean, with good beds and blackout curtains.

The restaurant is the surprise — the chef has Moroccan roots, and the dinner menu blends Icelandic ingredients with North African spicing. It's unusual and very good.

Good to know

Check-in: 3:00 PM (15:00). Check-out: 11:00 AM (11:00).

Breakfast: Included. Served in the restaurant with fjord views.

Dinner: The Moroccan menu is the draw. Open 6:00–9:00 PM (18:00–21:00). Reservations recommended — the restaurant is small and popular with locals. Ask at the desk or call ahead.

Parking: Free lot adjacent to the hotel.

Wi-Fi: Free.

Icelandic Culture Note

The name Siglunes means "mast point" — a reference to the ships that once crowded this harbor.

🇮🇸 Culture Note
In the herring boom years, the harbor at Siglufjörður was so packed with boats that you could walk across the masts from one side to the other — or so the old-timers claim. Siglu is related to sigla (to sail), and nes means point or peninsula. The hotel sits on the exact spot where fishing boats once tied up to unload herring by the ton.

The Story Behind It

The hotel is run by a local family who grew up in Siglufjörður. When the fish processing plant closed, they saw an opportunity: the building was solid, the location was perfect, and the town needed more beds. They opened in 2014 with 12 rooms and kept expanding. The Moroccan dinner menu came from a family connection — the chef married into the family and brought her grandmother's recipes.

We kept the concrete floors from the old plant. You can still see where the fish bins scraped the surface. My grandfather would gut herring standing exactly where the dining room is now. I like to think he'd approve — the food is better, and nobody has to stand in brine all day.
