Day 9

Arnarstapi

A tiny fishing village on the south shore of the Snæfellsnes peninsula — basalt columns, a sea arch, and a cliff-edge footpath to the next village that's one of the most scenic short walks in Iceland.

📍Snæfellsnes Peninsula
Directions Photos

What it is

Arnarstapi is a small, weather-beaten village at the foot of Snæfellsjökull — the glacier-topped volcano that Jules Verne made famous as the entrance to the center of the Earth. The village itself is barely a cluster of houses. The draw is the coastline: a lava field runs directly into the sea, the waves have carved the rock into a natural arch called Gatklettur, and the basalt columns stack up like organ pipes. Kittiwakes, fulmars, and Arctic terns nest on the cliffs in summer and will dive at anything they consider a threat — including your head.

Good to know

The path to Hellnar is the point. The trail is mostly flat, follows the cliff edge, and takes about 45 minutes one way. The rock underfoot is uneven lava — wear shoes with actual grip. There is no railing for long sections. Watch your step.

Don't wear a hat if you mind terns. Arctic terns nest on the cliffs from May to August. They are fiercely territorial and will dive-bomb walkers, sometimes striking the head. A walking stick held above your head is the traditional defense. Locals do not apologize for the birds — they were there first.

The cafe in the village is solid. There's a small cafe next to the harbor with coffee, soup, and waffles. The harbor wall is a good place to sit if the wind lets you.

Go early or late. The light on the basalt is best in the first hour after sunrise and the last hour before sunset. Midday is flat and the cliffs lose their color. The village is also nearly empty outside the summer coach-bus window of 11am–2pm.

Icelandic Culture Note

The cliffs at Arnarstapi are named in the Bárðar saga Snæfellsáss — one of the Icelandic sagas — as the home of a half-human, half-troll guardian of the peninsula.
🇮🇸The Guardian of Snæfellsnes
Bárður Snæfellsás was, according to the saga, the son of a human mother and a troll father. He settled at Arnarstapi, founded a settlement, and after death became the guardian spirit of the Snæfellsnes peninsula — protecting travelers and punishing the dishonest. A bronze statue of Bárður by sculptor Ragnar Kjartansson stands on the rocky shore, looking out to sea. Icelanders don't all believe in huldufólk (hidden people), but the cultural weight is real. You don't take the saga figures as jokes. The cliffs at Arnarstapi are part of the same landscape that Bárður was said to walk.

The Story Behind It

The black rock at Arnarstapi is the cooled flow from Snæfellsjökull — the glacier on the volcano visible to the north. The eruption that produced it was one of many that built the Snæfellsnes volcanic system, the same system that made the columns at Kirkjufellsfoss a few miles east. The natural arch called Gatklettur is a collapsed lava tube: the outer wall of a long-vanished tunnel fell into the sea, leaving just the entrance standing. The waves will finish the job eventually. Iceland's coastline loses about an acre of land to the sea every few years in the worst-hit places.
""You walk along a cliff with the Atlantic slamming into the rock below and fulmars wheeling overhead, and somewhere in your head the saga is still being told. Iceland is one of the few places on Earth where the landscape comes with a manuscript attached.""

The fishing station at Arnarstapi was active from the 1700s. The harbor is natural — a gap in the lava field that boats could use. By the mid-20th century, the cod stocks had shifted and most families moved away. The village today is mostly summer homes and a few working operations. The basalt, of course, doesn't care. It was here long before Bárður and will be here long after the cafe closes for the season.
