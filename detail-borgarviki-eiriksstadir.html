Day 8

Borgarvíki & Eiríksstaðir

Two stops on the Dalir coast that connect you to the Viking age. Borgarvíki is a dramatic red-sand cove with a natural stone fortress. Eiríksstaðir is the reconstructed longhouse of Erik the Red — the man who named Greenland and whose son, Leif Erikson, reached North America 500 years before Columbus.

📍 Dalir region, 450 Sælingsdalstunga
Directions Photos

What it is

Borgarvíki: A small, sheltered cove with a striking red sand beach and a natural rock formation that resembles a fortress wall. It was used as a seasonal fishing station for centuries. The red color comes from iron-rich volcanic rock. It's a 5-minute detour off Route 60 and worth it for the photo alone.

Eiríksstaðir: A reconstructed Viking longhouse based on the excavated foundations of the farm where Erik the Red lived in the 10th century. Erik was exiled from Iceland for murder, sailed west, and named the new land "Greenland" to attract settlers. His son Leif Erikson later sailed even further west and reached Newfoundland around 1000 AD — the first European in the Americas.

Good to know

Eiríksstaðir hours: Open June–August, 9:00 AM (09:00) – 6:00 PM (18:00). Entry 2,000 ISK (~$15). Guided tours run every 30 min.

The longhouse: Built to the exact dimensions of the original (excavated in the 1930s). The turf walls, wooden beams, and central fire pit are all authentic reconstructions. You can walk inside, sit on the benches, and get a real sense of Viking domestic life.

Driving: Both stops are on the Dalir coast road (Route 60). Borgarvíki is about 20 min north of Eiríksstaðir. Total time for both: 1–1.5 hours.

No food: The nearest restaurant is in Bústaðir or Reykholt. Pack a lunch if you're stopping here.

Icelandic Culture Note

Erik the Red was exiled from Iceland for murder — twice. His response was to discover and colonize Greenland.

🇮🇸 Culture Note
Erik Thorvaldsson, called "the Red" for his hair and temper, was not a man who handled conflict well. He killed two men in disputes, was declared an outlaw, and spent three years exploring the west. He returned to Iceland, recruited settlers with the brilliant marketing name "Greenland," and established a colony there in 985 AD. His son Leif Erikson, raised between Greenland and this exact farm in Iceland, sailed further west around 1000 AD and reached Vinland — modern-day Newfoundland. The reconstruction at Eiríksstaðir lets you stand in the kind of house where that history was set in motion.

The Story Behind It

The original farm was discovered in the 1930s when foundations were found under the turf. The reconstruction was completed in 2000, using the same materials and techniques as the 10th-century original. The turf walls are replaced every few years. The wooden beams are hand-hewn. The fire pit burns the same kind of wood. It's not a museum in the modern sense — it's an attempt to rebuild a house the way the original builders would have recognized it.

You duck through the low doorway and suddenly you're inside a Viking longhouse. The fire pit in the center, the wooden benches along the walls, the smoke hole in the roof. It smells like turf and wood smoke. You think: Erik the Red sat here. His son grew up here. And then he sailed west and found America. In this house.
