Day 9

Víðgelmir Lava Cave

One of the largest lava tubes in Iceland — 1,585 meters long, up to 16 meters tall, with ice formations that survive into summer and lava stalactites that look like dripping candle wax frozen mid-fall. The only major lava cave in Iceland you can only enter with a guide.

📍 Hallmundarhraun lava field, Borgarfjörður
Directions Photos

What it is

Víðgelmir (pronounced VITH-gel-mir) is the largest of Iceland's accessible lava tubes — a hollow tube of rock formed when the surface of a lava flow cooled and hardened while molten lava kept flowing underneath. When the eruption stopped, the molten river drained out, leaving an empty stone pipe. The Hallmundarhraun lava field that contains it erupted roughly 1,100 years ago, in 900–930 AD.

The cave is 1,585 meters long. The walking tour covers about 350 meters of the main passage — the rest is restricted to preserve the cave's fragile formations. Inside, you'll find lava stalactites (some still dripping — the cave is "active" in the geological sense), lava falls, ice columns that persist year-round in the cold section, and a remarkable color gradient in the rock: black where it was hottest, red and orange where iron-rich minerals oxidized.

Getting There

From the Snæfellsnes peninsula: Drive back across the mountain pass to Borgarnes, then take Route 50 (Borgarfjarðarbrú) and follow signs to Húsafell. From Húsafell, Víðgelmir is about 6 km further into the lava field. Total drive from Arnarstapi: ~2 hours.

From Reykjavík: Allow about 2 hours via Route 1 to Borgarnes, then Route 50. Most visitors combine this stop with the Húsafell Canyon Baths (15 minutes away) and continue to Reykjavík the same day.

From the ring road: Look for the small but clear-signed turnoff near Húsafell. The final stretch is on a gravel road — well-graded, fine for any rental car.

You cannot visit the cave independently. The entrance is gated, and the only way in is with a guide from The Cave operator. The tour is 45–60 minutes and runs year-round, weather permitting.

Good to know

Wear sturdy shoes. The path inside is uneven basalt and can be wet. The cave is 0–4°C year-round. The tour operator provides helmets with headlamps — that's the only gear you need.

The entrance is a series of spiral staircases. About 50 steps down through a man-made tunnel. Manageable for most people, but worth knowing if you have knee or balance issues.

Photography is fine but tricky. The cave is dimly lit for safety and formation preservation. A phone camera will struggle; a mirrorless or DSLR with a fast lens will do much better. No flash allowed.

Combine with Húsafell. The Canyon Baths are 15 minutes down the road. A standard day is lava cave at 12:00, lunch in Húsafell, canyon baths at 14:30, then drive back to Reykjavík.

Icelandic Culture Note

The Hallmundarhraun lava field takes its name from Hallmundur, a legendary 11th-century chieftain who, according to the Saga of Hallmundur, was given the lava field by a local farmer as thanks for some service — a payment that, in the words of the saga, "neither he nor his heirs ever came to regret." The implication: the land was considered worthless. Today it's a national treasure with a paved road, a guided tour, and a queue of visitors every summer.

🇮🇸 Why This Cave Is Gated
Before 1993, Víðgelmir was open to anyone. A combination of souvenir hunters chipping off lava formations, the cave's natural fragility, and a fatal accident involving a tourist triggered the government to gate it. The Cave operator — the only licensed guide company — was established specifically to allow access while protecting the site. Every visitor pays for that protection, and every visitor gets something most lava caves in the world can't offer: formations that are still growing, still dripping, still changing.
