Day 8

Kolugljúfur Canyon

A narrow, deep canyon with a waterfall that most tourists drive right past. The Víðidalsá river cuts through a basalt gorge, dropping into a series of pools. Easy to reach, rarely crowded, and one of the best-kept secrets on the Ring Road.

📍 Víðidalur, 560 Húnabyggð
Directions Photos

What it is

A basalt canyon about 1 km long and up to 25 meters deep, carved by the Víðidalsá river. The main waterfall — Kolufoss — drops in a single curtain into a deep pool at the head of the canyon. There's a second waterfall downstream, visible from a different vantage point along the rim trail.

The canyon is named after a troll woman named Kola who, according to legend, lived in the canyon and fished in the river. The viewing areas are right off Route 715 — no hike required. This is a 20-minute stop that punches well above its weight.

Good to know

Access: Turn off Route 1 onto Route 715 (towards Víðidalstungu). The canyon parking area is about 5 km down the road. Gravel road, passable for any car.

Viewing: Two fenced viewpoints on the canyon rim. The first gives you the main waterfall; the second shows the lower falls and the full length of the canyon. Both are a 2-minute walk from the car.

No facilities. No restrooms, no services. Go before you arrive.

Safety: Stay behind the fences. The canyon walls are undercut and the rock is slippery when wet. People have fallen here.

Icelandic Culture Note

The canyon is named after Kola, a troll woman from local folklore who supposedly fished for salmon in the river using her bare hands.

🇮🇸 Culture Note
In Icelandic folklore, trolls are not the bumbling giants of English fairy tales — they're ancient, powerful, and dangerous. Kola was said to live in the canyon, and the deep pools were her fishing spots. When the sun rose, she was turned to stone — as trolls are. Some say the rock formations at the canyon's edge are her hands, still reaching for the water. Whether you believe that or not, the canyon has a presence that makes the story feel less absurd than it sounds.

The Story Behind It

Kolugljúfur was largely unknown to tourists until about 10 years ago. It was a local spot — families would drive out on summer evenings, set up a picnic, and watch the waterfall. Social media changed that. Now it appears on "hidden gems" lists, though it's still far less crowded than the south coast waterfalls. The farm next door has been here since the 1800s; the farmer's family owns the land and maintains the viewing fences.

We almost drove past it. The sign is small and easy to miss. But we pulled over, walked 30 seconds, and there was this massive canyon with a waterfall right in front of us. No crowds, no parking fee, no gift shop. Just the canyon and the sound of water.
