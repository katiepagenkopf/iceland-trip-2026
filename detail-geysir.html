Geysir

Where the word 'geyser' was born

📍Haukadalur Valley
Directions Photos

What it is

The Great Geysir has gone quiet, but its neighbor Strokkur erupts every 5 to 10 minutes, hurling a column of boiling water up to 65 feet into the air. The surrounding Haukadalur valley is a restless geothermal field of bubbling mud pots, steaming fumaroles, and smaller hot springs — a live geology lesson in slow motion.

Good to know

Stand upwind of Strokkur — the spray soaks everything within 30 feet. A short loop trail circles the whole geyser field in about 20 minutes. The site is free, open 24/7, and lit at night in the off-season.

Icelandic Culture Note

A word borrowed by every language on Earth
🇮🇸The Name That Traveled
The English word 'geyser' comes straight from this one spring. Icelanders use 'geysa' — to gush. The word was first published in English in 1814, and the rest of the world adopted it for any erupting hot spring, from Yellowstone to New Zealand.

The Story Behind It

In 1845, an earthquake jolted Geysir back to life and it shot water roughly 170 feet into the air — taller than a 15-story building. Today it sleeps, but Strokkur next door keeps the family name alive.
"It throws up water with such violence that the roar is heard at a great distance."
