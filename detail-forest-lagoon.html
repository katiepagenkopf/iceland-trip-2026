Day 7

Forest Lagoon

A geothermal spa built into a birch forest above Akureyri, opened in 2022. Two infinity pools looking out over Eyjafjörður fjord, plus a cold plunge, sauna, and Iceland's first swim-up bar.

📍Akureyri
Directions Photos

What it is

Forest Lagoon is Akureyri's first major geothermal spa, built in 2022 into the existing Vaðlaskógur birch forest a few miles outside town. The setting is the unusual part: instead of a lava-field lagoon or a clifftop ocean view, the pools sit inside a stand of birch trees, with infinity edges looking out over Eyjafjörður fjord and the mountains on the far side.

There are two infinity pools (one hot, one slightly cooler), a cold plunge fed from a fresh-water spring, a sauna, and a swim-up bar — the first of its kind in an Icelandic lagoon. It's significantly less crowded than the lagoons on the south coast.

Good to know

Opens late. Unlike most Icelandic lagoons, Forest Lagoon doesn't open at the crack of dawn — typically 11am or noon. Check before you drive out.

Less crowded. Far quieter than Sky Lagoon, Blue Lagoon, or Mývatn Nature Baths.

The swim-up bar. Beer, wine, and soft drinks served to you in the pool. First of its kind in Iceland.

Cold plunge. The cold pool is fed from a fresh-water spring, not the fjord — it stays cold year-round.

What to bring. Swimsuit, towel (rental available), and a water bottle.

Icelandic Culture Note

Iceland's "forests" are surprisingly small by global standards, and the Forest Lagoon sits in one of the largest of them.

🇮🇸"Forest" is a Generous Term
Iceland has very few trees — deforestation began with the Viking settlement in the 9th century, when settlers cut down nearly everything for fuel, building material, and to clear land for sheep. By the early 20th century, forest cover was down to about 1% of the country. Today, an Icelandic "forest" is officially defined as any group of trees taller than a person — the bar is that low. The birch forest at Forest Lagoon is one of the larger stands in the country, and most of the trees are still only about 30 feet tall.

The Story Behind It

Forest Lagoon opened in 2022 as Akureyri's answer to the south-coast lagoons. It was deliberately built into the existing Vaðlaskógur forest — one of the few large birch forests in Iceland — rather than clearing trees to put in pools. The water comes from the same geothermal system that heats the town of Akureyri.

"You're soaking in 104°F water, drinking a beer, looking out at a fjord, surrounded by a forest. In Iceland, that's exotic."

The Icelandic Forest Service (Skógræktin) has been planting trees across the country since 1899 to reverse the Viking-era deforestation. Iceland is aiming for 12% forest cover by 2100, up from about 2% today. A century of work and the trees are still only ankle-deep by global standards.
