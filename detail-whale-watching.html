Whale Watching from Húsavík

Húsavík is the whale watching capital of Europe. Humpback whales are the stars — curious, often approaching the boats, and breaching spectacularly. North Sailing is the main operator.

What it is

Húsavík is a fishing town on the north coast of Iceland, sitting on Skjálfandi Bay — one of the richest whale-feeding grounds in the North Atlantic. The combination of cold Arctic currents, warm Gulf Stream water, and deep underwater canyons brings whales within sight of the harbour in summer. Humpbacks are the star attraction: they're curious, often approach the boats, and breach (leap fully out of the water) on a regular basis.

North Sailing is the main operator, running traditional oak boats converted from old fishing schooners. Tours typically last 2.5–3 hours, with a near-100% sighting rate in summer.

Whales seen
Humpback, minke, blue, orca, white-beaked dolphin
Tour length
2.5–3 hours

Good to know

Dress warm. Even in summer the wind off the bay is cold. Bring a waterproof jacket and a hat.

Seasickness. The bay is usually calm, but if you're prone, take dramamine 30 minutes before departure.

Old boats. The North Sailing fleet are traditional Icelandic oak boats — they look like fishing schooners, not tourist catamarans. Heel slightly in a swell.

Add the museum. The Húsavík Whale Museum is excellent and a natural pairing.

Icelandic Culture Note

The town has a second claim to fame that's a lot more fun than it sounds. The story starts with a movie.

🇮🇸Dinosaur Capital
Húsavík is also the self-declared "Dinosaur Capital of Iceland" — a tongue-in-cheek title the town adopted after the Eurovision Song Contest movie (2020) featured a fictional Eurovision contestant from Húsavík whose signature song was about dinosaurs. The town leaned into it: there's a dinosaur-themed walking trail, a small dinosaur museum at the harbour, and dinosaur sculptures in the streets. It's become a genuine tourist draw, especially for families.

The Story Behind It

Whale watching replaced commercial whaling as Húsavík's main industry. The Húsavík Whale Station processed whales from 1895 to 1984, when Iceland's commercial whaling moratorium took effect. Within a decade, former whalers were running whale-watching tours on the same waters. It's one of the clearest examples in the world of an industry reinventing itself after the resource it depended on was protected.

"You don't see whales because you got lucky. You see them because the bay is one of the richest feeding grounds in the North Atlantic."

Húsavík has the only wooden church in Iceland from the 19th century — Húsavíkurkirkja, built in 1907. Almost every other Icelandic church from that era is made of stone or concrete, because wood was already scarce on the treeless island.
