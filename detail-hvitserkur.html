Day 8

Hvítserkur

A 15-meter basalt sea stack rising from the waves off the Vatnsnes peninsula — a natural sculpture that looks like a dragon drinking from the sea, or a rhino, or a troll frozen mid-petrification. The most photographed rock formation in northern Iceland.

📍 Vatnsnes Peninsula, Northwest Iceland
Directions Photos

What it is

Hvítserkur (pronounced KVIT-ser-ker, "white shirt") is a dramatic basalt sea stack standing about 15 meters (49 feet) tall in Húnafjörður fjord. Unlike many Icelandic landmarks, it's only accessible at low tide — at high tide the stack is fully surrounded by water and the swell crashes against its base.

The rock is riddled with basalt columns, the result of slow cooling lava that fractured into hexagonal pillars. Over centuries, the sea has eroded softer material around the columns, leaving a jagged, almost architectural shape. People see different things in it: a dragon sipping from the ocean, a rhino, a kneeling figure, a troll caught by the sunrise and turned to stone. The name "white shirt" refers to the white guano streak that runs down its side from the seabirds nesting on top.

Getting There

From the Ring Road (Route 1): Turn off at the village of Hvammstangi onto Route 711, then follow signs to Vatnsnes peninsula and Hvítserkur. The drive from the ring road turn-off to the Hvítserkur parking lot is about 25 minutes.

Parking: A small paved lot sits about 200 meters from the cliff viewpoint. From the lot, a marked path leads down toward the sea — about 5 minutes walking one-way. There's no paved trail right up to the rock itself; you view it from a fenced clifftop area.

Combine with Illugastaðir seal beach: The two are a few minutes apart by car on Vatnsnes. Illugastaðir has a proper walk down to a black-sand beach where harbor seals haul out — most visitors do both in the same stop.

Check the tide: Low tide reveals more of the surrounding reef and lets you walk further along the beach. The rock looks taller and more isolated at high tide. Apps like Tide Charts (free) give accurate times for Hvammstangi.

Good to know

Stay back from the cliff edge. The basalt here is fractured and the drop to the water is sharp. There have been fatal falls in recent years. Stay on the marked path and well behind any fencing.

Bring a telephoto lens if you have one. From the cliff viewpoint, the rock is across a narrow channel — closer than it looks, but a 70–200mm lens will get you tight, dramatic frames of the basalt columns and the bird life on top.

Seal colony next door. Vatnsnes is one of the best places in Iceland to see harbor seals from shore. The Illugastaðir beach (5 minutes away) is more reliable for seal viewing than Hvítserkur itself.

No facilities at the site. There's a small outhouse at the parking lot, no café, no shop. Stock up on coffee and snacks in Hvammstangi before heading out.

Icelandic Culture Note

Iceland has a deep tradition of sagnakeðjur — "saga chains" — where one story's ending becomes the next's beginning. Hvítserkur features in a few regional tales, most commonly as the petrified remains of a troll who failed to make it to church before sunrise. The motif recurs across Iceland: trolls who lost their bet against Christianity and now stand silent in the landscape as a reminder that the old world was, quite literally, turned to stone.

🇮🇸 The Reinforced Stack
In the 1960s, the base of Hvítserkur was starting to erode. The landowner reinforced it by pouring concrete into the lower section to stop the sea from undercutting the columns. So the lower third of Iceland's most-photographed sea stack is, in fact, man-made. It's a rare example of Icelanders deciding that geology needed a helping hand.
