Day 3

Fjaðrárgljúfur

A serpentine, moss-walled canyon about 1.2 miles long and 330 feet deep — cut by glacial meltwater, winding through a flat plateau in an S-curve. A walking trail runs along the rim.

📍Near Kirkjubæjarklaustur
Directions Photos

What it is

Fjaðrárgljúfur is a long, narrow canyon in South Iceland, about 4 miles west of the village of Kirkjubæjarklaustur off Route 1. The canyon snakes through the landscape in a deep S-curve, with near-vertical walls covered in bright green moss. A trail runs along the top from a small parking area; it takes 40–60 minutes to walk the canyon rim end-to-end. The views down into the slot are the attraction. The floor of the canyon is private land and closed to visitors.

Good to know

It can be closed. The trail is shut during and after heavy rain — the rim path turns to mud and the erosion damage is significant. Check road.is or the local Kirkjubæjarklaustur tourist info before you go. There is no warning sign at the trailhead if it has been closed; you'll just find a rope across the gate.

Walk to the upper viewpoint. The most photographed angle is at the far (upstream) end of the trail, looking back down the S-curve. Most visitors turn around at the first overlook, 5 minutes in. The full walk takes about 45 minutes each way. Wear shoes you don't mind getting muddy.

It's a side trip from Route 1. The turnoff is just west of Kirkjubæjarklaustur. The canyon adds about 90 minutes round trip from the Ring Road, including the walk. Combine it with a Skaftafell or Vík stop on the same day.

Best light is overcast. Soft light brings out the green of the moss. Harsh midday sun flattens everything; golden hour makes the canyon walls glow but the canyon floor goes into shadow.

Icelandic Culture Note

This canyon was quiet for about two million years — then Justin Bieber filmed a music video there in 2015 and visitor numbers jumped by an order of magnitude.
🇮🇸When the World Discovered a Canyon
Fjaðrárgljúfur was a known local stop. The kind of place you might mention to a friend but that didn't make the international lists. Then in 2015, Justin Bieber released the music video for 'I'll Show You,' filmed largely at the canyon. The video has more than 500 million views. The canyon got more visitors in the next year than it had in the previous century. The trail, never designed for that load, started to erode. Iceland's environmental agency closed the canyon for part of 2016 and again in 2019 to let the path recover. The pattern is now common in Iceland's most photogenic places: a viral image, a flood of visitors, a temporary closure, a reopening with new rules. Iceland is a country that has had to learn, in real time, how to handle being beautiful.

The Story Behind It

The rock the canyon cuts through is about two million years old — laid down as volcanic sediment during an active phase of the Katla volcano system to the south. The canyon itself is much younger. At the end of the last ice age, about 9,000–10,000 years ago, the retreating glaciers released enormous volumes of meltwater. The Fjaðrá river cut down through the soft volcanic layers, but the river didn't go straight — it followed weaknesses and joints in the rock, producing the curving S-shape. The canyon is still being cut, slowly. The walls are tall enough that you're looking down on pine trees growing on the canyon floor.
""It is one of the few places in Iceland where the moss is louder than the water. The canyon is silent, green, and just deep enough to feel like a secret. The fact that several hundred million people have now seen it on YouTube hasn't really changed the canyon. It has just changed the parking lot.""

The land around Fjaðrárgljúfur has been used for sheep grazing for centuries. The trail and parking lot are on the high plateau; the canyon floor is still grazed in summer. You can sometimes see sheep at the bottom, looking very small.
