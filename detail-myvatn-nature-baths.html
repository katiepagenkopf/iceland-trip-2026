Day 5

Mývatn Nature Baths

A milky-blue geothermal lagoon in the remote north — the same mineral-rich water as the Blue Lagoon, but cheaper, less crowded, and with views across the volcanic Mývatn landscape.

📍Mývatn, Northeast Iceland
Directions Photos

What it is

The Mývatn Nature Baths (Jarðböðin in Icelandic, "the earth baths") are a milky-blue geothermal lagoon on the edge of Lake Mývatn in northeast Iceland. The water is the same temperature range as the Blue Lagoon (96–104°F), comes from the same kind of underground source, and is rich in the same minerals — sulfur, silicates, blue-green algae.

What Mývatn offers that the Blue Lagoon doesn't: fewer tourists, lower entry price, and a setting that looks out across an actual volcanic landscape (the Krafla caldera is visible from the pool) rather than a manicured resort.

Good to know

Price. Roughly half what the Blue Lagoon charges. Entry typically lands around $50 vs. $100+ at Blue Lagoon.

The smell. You'll notice the sulfur — that's how you know the water is the real thing. It can dry out your hair, so rinse thoroughly after your soak.

What to bring. Swimsuit, towel, and a water bottle. Towel rental available.

Sunset. The milky water turns pink at sunset — prime photo time.

Pair it. Add the Mývatn area drive: pseudo-craters, Dimmuborgir lava fields, Grjótagjá cave (the Game of Thrones cave).

Icelandic Culture Note

The lake next to the baths gave the entire region its name — and the name is a warning about summer in this part of Iceland.

🇮🇸Midge Lake
Mývatn means "midge lake" in Icelandic, named for the swarms of tiny flies that blanket the area in summer. The midges don't bite and they don't carry disease, but they will swarm around your face, get in your food, crawl into your ears, and cover every surface. Locals joke that the midges outnumber humans in the area about 100,000 to 1. The fish in the lake eat them by the ton — which is why the lake is also one of the best trout-fishing spots in the country.

The Story Behind It

The Mývatn area sits on top of the Krafla volcanic system, which erupted nine times between 1975 and 1984 in an event known as the "Krafla fires." The Nature Baths are heated by the same geothermal system that powers the Krafla geothermal power station, one of the largest in Iceland.

"It's the Blue Lagoon's quieter, cheaper, more authentic cousin — and the midges make sure you don't forget you're in the wild."

The milky blue color comes from dissolved silicates and blue-green algae, exactly the same chemistry as the Blue Lagoon 300 miles south. Icelanders will quietly tell you Mývatn was here first, and the Blue Lagoon copied the formula. They are not wrong.
