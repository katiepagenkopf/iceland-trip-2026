Day 8

Guðrúnarlaug Hot Pot

A small natural hot pool in the middle of nowhere, named after Guðrún Ósvífsdóttir — the most famous woman in medieval Icelandic literature. The water is geothermal and the setting is straight out of a saga: stone walls, mossy turf, and a stream running through the valley below.

📍 Guðrúnarlaug, Húnaþing vestra
Directions Photos

What it is

Guðrúnarlaug is a small, stone-lined hot pool fed by a natural hot spring, sitting in a quiet green hollow on a remote farm in Húnaþing vestra, northern Iceland. It's a real hot pot — not a polished spa, not a tourist attraction with changing rooms, just a working piece of Icelandic bathing culture that happens to be 1,000 years old.

The pool is named after Guðrún Ósvífsdóttir, the central character of the Laxdæla saga — one of the great works of medieval Icelandic literature. According to the saga, this is where she used to bathe, and it has been a place of local gathering ever since. There's a stone marker by the pool, and a small changing hut on the bank.

Getting There

From Vatnsnes / Hvammstangi: Head south on Route 711, then turn onto Route 716. The site is signed from Route 716 — look for a small parking area and a path leading down to the stream. Drive time from Hvammstangi is roughly 30 minutes.

From Borgarnes (on your way to/from Snæfellsnes): Allow about 1.5 hours. It's a good half-way stop if you're going between the Vatnsnes peninsula and the Snæfellsnes peninsula.

Parking: Small gravel pullout next to a farm. The pool is a 1–2 minute walk from the parking area, down a gentle slope to the stream.

Facilities: A small wooden changing hut sits next to the pool. It's basic but functional. No showers, no café, no reception desk.

Good to know

The water is genuinely hot. Unlike many tourist hot pots that are tempered to 38°C, Guðrúnarlaug can run 40–42°C at the source end. Test the water before you sink in, and stay closer to the inflow (warmer) or the outflow (cooler) depending on your preference.

Bring your own towel and swimsuit. There are no rentals on site. A few hooks and a small bench in the changing hut, but that's it.

The pool is small — 2 to 4 people is comfortable. More than that and you're shoulder-to-shoulder. If you arrive and someone's already in, give them space or wait.

Respect the honor box. Maintenance is paid for by visitor donations. There's a small box near the changing hut — drop a few hundred krónur in if you can.

It's not a developed site. Don't expect a Blue Lagoon experience. This is a quiet, mossy, slightly imperfect farm pool that happens to be one of the most literary hot pots in the world.

Icelandic Culture Note

Guðrún Ósvífsdóttir (c. 934 – c. 1000 AD) is the protagonist of the Laxdæla saga — the most-celebrated love story in the Old Norse canon. She had four marriages, multiple lovers, and was famous for both her beauty and her fury. After her beloved Kjartan was killed, she reportedly ate a heart (theirs or someone else's, depending on the manuscript) and lived out her days as a nun.

🇮🇸 Why Icelanders Painted Her on a Coin
Guðrún appeared on the Icelandic 10,000 króna banknote from 2013 to 2021 — making her one of the few medieval literary characters in the world with a national currency portrait to her name. When the series was redesigned, Icelanders protested losing her from the bill. The hot pot named after her has been a working bath for at least 1,000 years, possibly longer — which means the people of Húnaþing vestra have been soaking in the same water since before the country had a written legal code.
