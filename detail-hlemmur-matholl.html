Day 1

Hlemmur Mathöll

Reykjavik's original food hall, inside a converted 1930s bus station on Laugavegur. Multiple vendors under one roof — Icelandic fish soup, Vietnamese bánh mì, wood-fired pizza, craft beer. Walk-in only, no reservations.

📍 Laugavegur 107, 101 Reykjavík
Directions Photos

What it is

A food hall in the old Hlemmur bus station, right at the top of Laugavegur. The building was Reykjavik's main bus terminal from 1930 until 2017, when it was converted into a market hall. The original concrete structure and arched ceiling are still visible — it feels like eating inside a piece of the city's transit history.

There are roughly 10 vendors. The standouts: SKÁL! for Icelandic fish soup and fermented everything, Büllu Smurbrauð for open-faced sandwiches, and Viet Noodle Bar for pho and bánh mì. Most mains run 1,800–3,200 ISK ($13–23).

Good to know

No reservations. Walk in, grab a seat at one of the communal tables. Busiest at lunch (12–2 PM) and again around 6–8 PM. If it's full, try the mezzanine level.

Cashless. All vendors take cards. No one accepts cash here.

Hours: 11:30 AM (11:30) – 9:00 PM (21:00) daily. Some vendors close earlier on Sundays.

Location: Right on Laugavegur, about a 10-minute walk downhill from Hotel Frón.

Icelandic Culture Note

The name Hlemmur means "lid" or "cover" — it originally referred to a covered drain grate at this intersection in the 19th century.

🇮🇸 Culture Note
Before it was a bus station, this corner of Laugavegur was just a muddy crossroads with a wooden grate covering a ditch. The grate was called hlemmur, and the name stuck to the whole area. When the bus terminal was built in 1930, it took the name. When the food hall replaced it in 2017, they kept it. Icelanders hold onto place names the way some families hold onto recipes — the origin doesn't have to make sense, it just has to be right.

The Story Behind It

The bus station was the beating heart of Reykjavik public transit for nearly 90 years. Every Icelander who grew up in the capital has a memory of waiting at Hlemmur — catching the #1 bus, meeting friends under the clock, eating a hot dog from the stand outside. When Strætó (the city bus company) moved operations to the new BSÍ terminal, there was genuine grief. The food hall conversion was controversial — some saw it as gentrification, others as saving a beloved building from demolition.

My grandmother used to catch the bus to Akureyri from here in the 1960s. Two days on the road. Now I come for a bowl of fish soup and a craft beer. She would have found that very funny.
