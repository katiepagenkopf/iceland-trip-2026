Þingvellir National Park

A rift valley where two continental plates pull apart — and where, in the year 930, Icelandic chieftains founded the Alþingi, one of the world's oldest parliaments. Walk between continents, swim between continents, stand where Viking-age laws were spoken aloud.

📍 Bláskógabyggð, Southwest Iceland
Directions Photos

What it is

A UNESCO World Heritage Site and Iceland's most important historical and geological place. The North American and Eurasian tectonic plates meet here — you can see the rift in the Almannagjá gorge, walk through it, and watch the land widening at about an inch every two years.

In 930 AD, Viking chieftains gathered here to form the Alþingi — an open-air parliament that met every summer for over 800 years. Laws were recited from memory at Lögberg (Law Rock) by an elected Lawspeaker. This is also where Iceland formally adopted Christianity in 1000 AD and declared independence from Denmark in 1944.

Good to know

Silfra fissure: The famous dive site is a crack in the lava filled with glacial meltwater filtered through decades of rock. Visibility exceeds 300 feet, and you're swimming literally between two tectonic plates. Drysuit required (the water stays around 36–39°F year-round). Tours depart from the Silfra parking area.

Walk the Almannagjá gorge: The main walking path runs the length of the gorge. It's about 1.5 miles one-way from the visitor center to Lögberg. Boardwalk all the way, with views down into the rift on both sides.

Öxarárfoss: The park's only major waterfall. A short walk from the main path; the water drops in a single curtain over the basalt cliff edge.

Free ranger walks: June through August, the park offers free guided walks in English — 10 AM from Þingvallakirkja church, 2 PM from the visitor center.

Icelandic Culture Note

The name Þingvellir literally means "Assembly Plains" — þing (assembly) + vǫllr (field). It's the same root that gives English the word "thing" (as in "the thing has been decided"). Icelandic is one of the few languages in the world where the word for "parliament" still looks and sounds like "people gathering in a field to talk."

🇮🇸 The Lawspeaker's Memory
For roughly 200 years after the Alþingi was founded, Iceland had no written laws. A man called the Lögsögumaður (Lawspeaker) was elected to a three-year term, and his job was to memorize the entire body of law — and recite it aloud at Lögberg every summer. To check that he still knew it, the assembly would ask him to recite specific passages. He was also expected to add new laws to his recitation. The Lawspeaker position was abolished in 1800. Iceland's commitment to oral tradition — and to believing that a society can govern itself by talking — runs straight through this rock.

The Story Behind It

At the time of the first parliament, the chieftain who owned this land had been outlawed for murder. His land was declared public — and the new Icelandic state simply used it. Two-thirds of the Icelandic nation would travel up to 17 days to attend. They set up temporary turf-and-cloth shelters, sold goods, played games, and settled feuds. In many years, the most consequential decisions in Icelandic history were made at this exact spot.

"Stand at Lögberg at dawn in summer and the silence is almost uncomfortable. The chieftains stood where you're standing. The Lawspeaker spoke from this rock. For 800 years this was the most powerful piece of ground in Iceland. It's still a national shrine, by law, that can never be sold."
