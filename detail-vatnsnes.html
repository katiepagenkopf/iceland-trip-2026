Day 8

Vatnsnes Peninsula

A wild, sparsely populated peninsula jutting into Húnaflói bay. Best known for its seal colonies — harbor seals haul out on the beaches year-round, and the viewing is some of the best in Iceland. Also home to Hvítserkur, the iconic sea stack.

📍 Vatnsnes, 560 Húnabyggð
Directions Photos

What it is

A peninsula that loops north from Hvammstangi into the Húnaflói bay. The ring road around it (Route 711) is about 90 km — partially paved, partially gravel. The peninsula is sheep farming country, with a few farms, a church, and wide-open views of the bay and the Westfjords across the water.

The main draw is seals. Harbor seals breed on the beaches here, and you can often see them from the road. The best viewing spots are at Hindisvík and the Illugastaðir farm, where a short path leads to a beach where seals are frequently hauled out. You're also driving past Hvítserkur, the 15-meter basalt sea stack that looks like a drinking dragon.

Good to know

Seal viewing: Best at low tide, when seals haul out on the rocks and beaches. They're used to people — stay quiet and you can get within 30–50 meters. Harbor seals are present year-round; gray seals appear in autumn.

Illugastaðir: A farm on the east coast of the peninsula with a dedicated seal viewing path. Small entry fee (500 ISK ~$4). The family who runs it puts out binoculars and information boards.

Driving: The full loop is about 2 hours with stops. The road is gravel for much of the northern section — go slowly, watch for sheep.

Hvítserkur: Visible from the road on the east side of the peninsula. There's a parking area with a steep path down to the beach. 15-minute stop.

Icelandic Culture Note

The peninsula's name means "lake nose" — vatn (water/lake) + nes (peninsula) — referring to the lakes that dot its interior.

🇮🇸 Culture Note
Vatnsnes is one of the few places in Iceland where you can watch seals in their natural habitat without a boat tour. Iceland's relationship with seals is complicated — they were hunted for centuries for their blubber and fur, and some fishermen still consider them pests. But attitudes are shifting. The Illugastaðir farm switched from sheep to seal tourism, and their viewing platform has become one of the most visited spots on the peninsula. The seals don't seem to mind.

The Story Behind It

The peninsula was once more populated than it is now. In the early 20th century, several farms operated along the coast, fishing and farming. The 1963 blizzard killed thousands of sheep across the north, and many Vatnsnes farmers never recovered. The land consolidated into fewer, larger farms. Today there are maybe 20 families on the whole peninsula. The emptiness is part of the appeal.

We sat on the rocks at Illugastaðir for 45 minutes, just watching the seals. There were maybe 15 of them, lying on the beach, sliding in and out of the water. One kept looking at us, like it was deciding whether we were interesting enough to worry about. We weren't.
