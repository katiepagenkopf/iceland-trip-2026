Day 1

Hallgrímskirkja

Reykjavík's 244-foot Lutheran church, the largest in Iceland. The façade is a deliberate echo of the basalt columns at Svartifoss — a 41-year build designed to outshine every skyline in the country.

📍 Hallgrímstorg 1, 101 Reykjavík
Directions Photos

What it is

A Lutheran parish church in the heart of Reykjavík and the tallest church in Iceland. Designed in 1937 by state architect Guðjón Samúelsson, the stepped façade is a direct imitation of the columnar basalt cliffs and "organ pipe" lava formations found across the country — most famously at Svartifoss waterfall in Skaftafell.

Construction took 41 years (1945–1986), and the architect died in 1950 without ever seeing it finished. The tower reaches 244 feet, and an elevator takes you most of the way up. From the top you can see the whole city, the bay, the mountains, and on a clear day, Snæfellsjökull glacier 60 miles to the west.

Good to know

Church entry is free. Tower access costs around 1,000 ISK (about $7), and tickets are sold at the gift shop inside. The elevator takes you to the 8th floor; a short staircase leads to the open-air viewing platform on the 9th.

Best light: Late afternoon in summer — the sun hangs low and gilds the white concrete. The same light makes the Leif Eriksson statue (a gift from the US in 1930) glow against the façade.

Concerts: The Klais organ is one of the few in the world played regularly. The summer organ series runs June–August; Advent concerts in December are candlelit and otherworldly.

Hidden detail: Stand at the base of the tower and look up. The columns are sized so the church looks taller the higher you go — a deliberate visual trick that makes the 244 feet feel like more.

Icelandic Culture Note

The church is named after Hallgrímur Pétursson, a 17th-century poet-priest whose Passíusálmar (Passion Hymns) are still the most quoted poetry in Iceland. Icelanders grew up reading his work in school and hearing it in church. Naming the country's largest church after him is roughly the equivalent of naming your national cathedral after your national poet.

🇮🇸 State Architect Guðjón Samúelsson
Samúelsson was Iceland's first formally trained architect and designed an astonishing number of the country's most recognizable buildings — Hallgrímskirkja, the National Theatre, Akureyri Church, the University of Iceland main building. He believed Icelandic architecture should imitate Icelandic geology. Walk into any bookshop in Reykjavík and you'll find at least one monograph on him. His central idea — that a country's buildings should look like the country itself — is still the dominant school of thought in Icelandic design.

The Story Behind It

The original design was shorter. The Church of Iceland's leaders wanted a spire tall enough to outshine Landakotskirkja, the Catholic cathedral in Reykjavík. The redesign added the full 244-foot tower — and a piece of quiet, mostly forgotten Icelandic religious politics was written into the skyline.

"Samúelsson didn't draw a church. He drew a piece of Iceland — the basalt cliffs, the glaciers, the columnar joints of cooled lava. He died before the steeple was even half done, but he knew it would work. The country is shaped like a church, so the church should be shaped like the country."
