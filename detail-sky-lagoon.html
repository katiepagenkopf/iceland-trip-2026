Day 1

Sky Lagoon

An oceanside geothermal spa in Kópavogur, just minutes from Reykjavík. The infinity edge meets the North Atlantic, the water stays a steady 100–104°F, and the seven-step Skjól ritual walks you through Iceland's bathing culture in one sitting.

📍 Kópavogur, near Reykjavík
Directions Photos

What it is

A modern geothermal spa on the edge of Kársnes Harbour in Kópavogur, just south of Reykjavík. The main draw is the infinity-edge lagoon — 230 feet of curved pool that seems to merge with the North Atlantic beyond. The water comes up from deep underground and holds a constant 100–104°F (38–40°C).

Sky Lagoon opened in 2021 and was designed to feel like a continuation of Iceland's landscape — turf walls, grey-blues, deep greens, and a panoramic sauna with a single sweeping window facing the ocean. The signature Skjól ritual moves you through seven steps: the lagoon, a cold plunge, the sauna, a cold fog mist, a body scrub, a steam room, and a final soak.

Good to know

The Skjól ritual: Worth the upcharge. The seven steps take about 90 minutes total and walk you through the rhythm of Icelandic hot-cold bathing — lagoon, cold plunge, sauna, cold fog, body scrub, steam, lagoon. The sauna window alone is worth the trip.

What to bring: Just yourself — towel, robe, slippers, and body products are included. Swimwear is required (rentals available).

Booking ahead: Time slots fill up, especially around sunset and on weekends. The Saman (shared) pass is the cheapest; the Sér (private) pass gives you a private changing cabin and longer in the lagoon.

Best light: Sunset turns the ocean copper and the steam glows. If you're going for photos, book the last time slot of the day.

Icelandic Culture Note

Going to the hot pot isn't a luxury in Iceland — it's a Tuesday. Public pools (sundlaugar) are subsidized by municipalities and are how Icelanders decompress, meet friends, gossip, and gossip about their friends. The hot pot conversation is its own social contract: someone asks where you're from, you ask about the weather, and the chat usually involves an opinion about a swimming pool.

🇮🇸 The Hot-Cold Rhythm
Icelanders don't sit in a hot pool for an hour straight. The pattern is hot, then cold, repeat — the cold plunge or cold shower wakes the body, tightens blood vessels, and actually makes the next round in the hot water feel better. The contrast is the point. The Skjól ritual at Sky Lagoon follows this same pattern: lagoon, cold plunge, sauna, cold mist, back to the lagoon. It's not spa pampering — it's how Icelanders have been bathing for centuries.

The Story Behind It

Sky Lagoon was designed by architect Halldór Eiríksson to echo the cliffs, turf houses, and ocean textures of Iceland. The grey-blue palette, the curving turf walls, the cave-like tunnel entrance — all of it is meant to feel rooted in this specific place, not like a generic luxury spa.

"The first infinity-edge pool I ever swam in was in Iceland, and it changed what I thought a spa could be. The trick isn't the water — the trick is the horizon. You sink into the warmth, look up, and realize the ocean hasn't moved an inch."
