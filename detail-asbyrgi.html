Day 6

Ásbyrgi Canyon

A horseshoe-shaped canyon with 300-ft walls, a birch forest at the bottom, and a pond at the end. Legend says it's the hoof print of Odin's eight-legged horse.

📍Vatnajökull National Park, North
Directions Photos

What it is

Ásbyrgi is a horseshoe-shaped canyon in the northern reaches of Vatnajökull National Park. The walls rise roughly 300 ft straight up on three sides, the floor is carpeted with birch and rowan trees, and a small pond (Botnstjörn) sits at the closed end. The shape is so clean and geometric it looks man-made.

There's a flat walking trail through the bottom of the canyon (about 1 mile each way), and a cliff-top viewpoint just off the parking lot that gives you the iconic full horseshoe photo. Easy walking, no scrambling required.

Canyon size
~2 miles long, 0.5 miles wide
Wall height
~300 ft
Forest
Downy birch and rowan
Mythology
Sleipnir's hoof print

Good to know

Two easy walks. The cliff-top viewpoint is 5 minutes from the parking lot. The floor trail to Botnstjörn pond is about 1 mile each way on flat ground.

Bring food. The pond at the end of the canyon is a perfect lunch spot with no crowds.

No services inside. No café, no restrooms past the visitor area — bring water and snacks.

Part of Vatnajökull. The canyon is in the Jökulsárgljúfur section of Vatnajökull National Park, which is huge — plan to combine with Dettifoss or Húsavík.

How to get there. From Mývatn, take Route 1 north toward Húsavík, then turn east on Route 85. Ásbyrgi is about 55 km (45 min) from Húsavík. Hljóðaklettar (echo rocks) is 20 km south of Ásbyrgi on Route 85 — stop there first if you're coming from the south.

Best light. Morning light hits the east walls clean; evening light catches the rim.

Icelandic Culture Note

The canyon's shape and its name both come from the same Norse myth. Geologists tell a different story. Both are true in their own way.

🇮🇸Shelter of the Gods
Ásbyrgi means "Shelter of the Gods" in Icelandic. According to legend, Sleipnir — Odin's eight-legged horse — touched down here while Odin was chasing the frost giants across the sky. The horseshoe-shaped canyon is the imprint of his hoof. Geologists tell a different story: the canyon was carved by catastrophic glacial floods at the end of the last ice age, around 10,000 years ago, when a volcanic eruption under a glacier released a wall of meltwater that ripped through the landscape. Both explanations describe the same shape.

The Story Behind It

The canyon was a standalone national park (Jökulsárgljúkur) until 2008, when it was folded into Vatnajökull National Park as part of Iceland's consolidation of protected areas. Before that, it was one of the most visited natural sites in northern Iceland.

"Geologists see a flood. The Norse saw a god's horse. Either way, it's the most dramatic canyon in Iceland."

The flat valley floor of Ásbyrgi is unusual — most Icelandic canyons are V-shaped (carved by rivers) or U-shaped (carved by glaciers). Ásbyrgi is uniquely horseshoe-shaped, which is what makes the myth plausible and the geology a puzzle.
