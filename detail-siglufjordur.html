Day 7

Siglufjörður

A fishing village tucked into a narrow fjord at the northern tip of Iceland. Once the herring capital of the North Atlantic — now a town of 1,300 with a world-class herring museum, painted wooden houses, and some of the best-preserved fishing heritage in the country.

📍 Siglufjörður, 580 Fjallabyggð
Directions Photos

What it is

Siglufjörður sits at the end of a narrow fjord, hemmed in by steep mountains on three sides. It's the northernmost town on the Icelandic mainland — you can't drive any further north without hitting the ocean. The town boomed in the early 20th century when herring stocks exploded off the north coast, and it shrank just as fast when the herring collapsed in the late 1960s.

Today it's a quiet, well-preserved village with colorful houses, a working harbor, and the Herring Era Museum — the largest maritime museum in Iceland. The drive in along Route 76 from Dalvík is one of the most scenic in the country: narrow road, sheer cliffs, tunnels through the mountains.

Good to know

Herring Era Museum: Open May–September, 10:00 AM (10:00) – 6:00 PM (18:00). Three restored factory buildings tell the story of the herring boom. Allow 1–2 hours. Entry 2,500 ISK (~$18).

Folk Music Center: A small museum in a restored house, dedicated to Icelandic folk music. Worth 30 minutes.

Driving: Route 76 from Dalvík is paved but narrow with single-lane tunnels. Drive slowly — oncoming traffic uses pullouts. About 45 min from Dalvík, 1.5 hours from Akureyri.

Supplies: Small Samkaup grocery store in town. Stock up in Akureyri if you need more.

Icelandic Culture Note

The herring boom made Siglufjörður the wealthiest town in Iceland per capita — for about 30 years, it was the place to be.

🇮🇸 Culture Note
In the 1930s–1960s, Siglufjörður was Iceland's boomtown. Norwegian and Icelandic fishermen followed the herring to the north coast, and the town swelled with workers, salting stations, and money. At its peak, 10,000 people lived and worked here during herring season — in a town built for 1,300. Women ran the salting stations, gutting and packing fish at industrial speed. The money built the town's grandest houses and funded Iceland's first swimming pool. When the herring vanished in 1968, the party ended.

The Story Behind It

The road to Siglufjörður used to be impassable in winter. Until the tunnels were blasted through the mountains in the 1980s and 2010s, the town was effectively cut off for months each year. That isolation shaped the community — self-reliant, tight-knit, suspicious of outsiders. Today the tunnels are open year-round, but the town still feels remote. That's the appeal.

The tunnels changed everything. Before them, you could only reach Siglufjörður by boat or over the mountain pass — and the pass closed in October. Some winters, the town went months without a supply truck. They grew vegetables in greenhouses heated by hot springs. When the tunnels finally opened, people cried. Not because they were glad to leave — because they were glad the rest of Iceland could finally reach them.
